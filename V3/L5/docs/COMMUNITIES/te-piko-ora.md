## 2. Name and Cosmology

### 2.1 Etymology

| Word | Language | Meaning |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Te** | Tahitian | The (definite article) |
| **Pīko** | Proto-Polynesian | Navel, umbilical cord, center, origin point |
| **Ora** | Tahitian | Life, health, well-being, salvation |

**Full meaning:** *"The Living Navel"* — the point from which all life flows outward, and to which all life returns.

### 2.2 The Piko Ritual

In traditional Polynesian birth practice:
1. The **piko** (umbilical cord) is cut with a bamboo knife
2. The **whenua** (placenta, literally "land") is buried
3. The **piko** is planted with a **tree** (often coconut or breadfruit)
4. The child is forever **connected to that tree and that land**
5. The tree becomes a **living ancestor**

This is the **blockchain of Polynesia** — an immutable, living record of origin, written not in code but in wood and leaf and fruit.

> *„Te Pīko Ora je místem, kde se piko každého návštěvníka — každého Guardiana — spojí s pokořením. Kde se strom života stane tvým předkem. Kde ZION není technologie, ale tanec."*

### 2.3 The Polynesian Genesis Block

In Tahitian cosmology, the universe began with **Ta'aroa** (the supreme deity) in a shell. He broke out, created the sky (*Rangi*), the earth (*Papa*), and all living things. But he needed **light** — so he created **Tāne** (god of forests and light), who separated earth and sky and brought the sun.

This is the **Polynesian Genesis**:
- **Ta'aroa** = The protocol (the invisible source, like Hunab Ku)
- **Tāne** = The node (the one who brings light, validates, connects)
- **The coconut palm** = The Tree of Life (provides everything, self-sustaining)
- **The canoe (*va'a*)** = The DAO (everyone must paddle together, no single captain)
- **The stars (*fetu'u*)** = Consensus (wayfinding by reading many signals, not one GPS)

## 3. Legal Structure

### 3.1 French Polynesia Context

| Aspect | Status | Details |
|--------|--------|---------|
| **Legal entity** | 🔵 Researching | French Polynesia is an overseas collectivity of France. Special status since 2004 (*Pays d'outre-mer*). |
| **Land ownership** | 🔵 Complex | Foreigners cannot directly own land in FP. Options: lease (emphyteutic lease up to 30 years), local partnership, or *société civile* with majority Polynesian partners. |
| **Residency** | 🔵 Required | Long-stay requires visa (French Schengen rules apply). Digital nomad visa exists but limited. |
| **Taxation** | 🔵 Favorable | French Polynesia has its own tax code (*Code des impôts de la Polynésie française*). No VAT. Import duties high. |
| **Environmental** | 🔵 Strict | FP has strong environmental protection laws. Building near lagoon requires *DUP* (déclaration d'utilité publique) or *permis d'aménager*. |

### 3.2 Recommended Legal Path

**Option A: Association Loi 1901 (French non-profit)**
- Pros: No minimum capital, tax-exempt if humanitarian/educational purpose, can lease land
- Cons: Cannot own land directly, foreign board members limited to 1/3
- **Best for:** Phase 0–2, community activities, retreats

**Option B: Société Civile Immobilière (SCI) with Local Partner**
- Pros: Can acquire land via long-term lease (*bail emphytéotique*)
- Cons: Requires trusted local partner (50%+ Polynesian ownership for some land types)
- **Best for:** Phase 2+, permanent land access

**Option C: Tahitian *mā'ohi* Partnership**
- Pros: Deep cultural integration, access to customary land (*tapis*)
- Cons: Complex customary law (*ra'au*), requires elder (*pule*) approval
- **Best for:** Long-term sustainability and cultural legitimacy

**Recommended:** Start with **Association Loi 1901** + build trust with local community for eventual **customary partnership**.

### 4.3 Food and Agriculture

#### 4.3.1 Land-Based Agroforestry

Polynesia already has the **ultimate food forest model**: the **mixed orchard** (*mara*).

| Layer | Species | Yield | Use |
|-------|---------|-------|-----|
| **Emergent** | Coconut palm (*nī*), breadfruit (*uru*), jackfruit | Year-round | Staple starch, oil, fiber |
| **Canopy** | Mango, avocado, soursop (*taporo*), citrus | Seasonal | Fresh, dried, juice |
| **Understory** | Banana (*me'i*), papaya (*nia*), taro (*poi*) | Year-round | Staple starch, leaves |
| **Shrub** | Pineapple (*painapo*), chili, vanilla, coffee | Seasonal | Spice, cash crop |
| **Ground** | Sweet potato (*umar*), cassava (*manioke*), turmeric (*renga*) | Year-round | Staple, medicine |
| **Aquatic** | Taro in flooded patches (*vao*) | Year-round | Traditional staple |
| **Vine** | Passion fruit (*pasiona*), cucumber, beans | Seasonal | Fresh, juice |

**Calorie self-sufficiency target:**
- Phase 1: 30%
- Phase 2: 60%
- Phase 3: 90% (Polynesian traditional diet is highly self-sufficient)

#### 4.3.2 Marine Permaculture (Unique to Te Pīko Ora)

This is what makes Te Pīko Ora different from Genesis Garden and Dharma Temple. It integrates **land and ocean** as one system.

| System | Phase 1 | Phase 2 | Phase 3 |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Fish ponds (*vāvā*)** | Traditional stone ponds restored | Expanded, integrated with taro patches | Closed-loop aquaculture |
| **Seawater farming** | None | Seaweed (*limu*) cultivation | Commercial seaweed + pearl oysters |
| **Pearl farming** | None | Small-scale (*pinctada margaritifera*) | Black pearl cooperative |
| **Lagoon aquaculture** | None | Fish traps (* Māori hīnaki* style) | Integrated reef restoration |

**The Coconut-Fish-Taro Triad:**
1. Coconut fronds → mulch → taro beds
2. Taro waste → fish pond feed
3. Fish pond water (nutrient-rich) → taro irrigation
4. Coconut husks → rope → fish traps
5. Fish → protein
6. Taro → starch
7. Coconut → fat + water

This is a **3,000-year-old permaculture system** — perfected by Polynesians long before the word "permaculture" existed.

## 5. The Tree of Life Manifestation

### 5.1 The Coconut Palm as Axis Mundi

In Te Pīko Ora, the **coconut palm (*Cocos nucifera*)** is not a crop. It is a **deity**.

| Part of Palm | Polynesian Name | Use | Sacred Meaning |
|--------------|----------------|-----|----------------|
| **Fruit / nut** | *Nī* | Food, water, oil, milk | The head — contains the "heart" (liquid embryo = immortality drink) |
| **Frond / leaf** | *Rau* | Roofing, baskets, shade | The arms — shelter, protection |
| **Trunk** | *Tūmū* | Timber, bridge, post | The spine — strength, connection between earth and sky |
| **Root** | *A'a* | Medicine, rope fiber | The feet — anchoring, grounding |
| **Husk / fiber** | *Pulu* | Rope, compost, fuel | The skin — protection, binding |
| **Shell** | *Ivi* | Bowl, cup, craft | The skull — container, memory |
| **Sap / toddy** | *Miti* | Fermented drink, vinegar | The blood — intoxication, communion |

**Ritual:** Every new Guardian plants a coconut palm with their **piko** (umbilical cord symbolism — a lock of hair or a personal token). The palm becomes their **living ancestor** in the community.

### 5.2 The Tatau as Living Ledger

Traditional Polynesian tattoo (*tatau*) is a **permanent record**:
- **Genealogy** (who are your ancestors)
- **Achievements** (what have you done)
- **Status** (what is your role)
- **Protection** (what spirits guard you)

In Te Pīko Ora, this becomes a metaphor for **on-chain identity**:
- Every Guardian receives a **symbolic tatau** (not necessarily skin — can be wood carving, digital art, or actual tattoo if desired)
- The design encodes: community entry date, role, contributions, lineage
- It is **immutable** — once given, it cannot be erased (like a blockchain transaction)
- It is **verified** — only the master tattooist (*tufuga ta tatau*) or community elders can authorize it

### 5.3 Wayfinding as Consensus

Polynesian navigation (*fa'atere*) is the art of sailing thousands of kilometers without instruments:
- **Stars** (*fetu'u*) — multiple reference points, not one GPS satellite
- **Waves** (*galu*) — reading patterns, not a single data stream
- **Birds** (*manu*) — local knowledge, not a centralized database
- **Clouds** (*ao*) — predictive, not reactive
- **Intuition** (*mauri*) — the navigator's inner knowing, like a validator's local state

**This is distributed consensus:** No single source of truth. All sources must agree before the canoe changes course.

In Te Pīko Ora, this is taught as a **meditation practice** and a **governance metaphor**:
- Before any major decision, the "navigator" (facilitator) reads multiple "signs" (perspectives)
- Only when stars, waves, birds, and intuition align does the community "change course"
- Disagreement is not failure — it is **missing data**. Wait. Watch. The sign will come.

## 8. Unique Rituals and Practices

### 8.1 The Genesis Planting
Every new Guardian, every new guest who stays 21+ days, plants a **coconut palm** with a personal token. The palm is named, catalogued, and cared for by the community forever.

### 8.2 The Wayfinding Council
Monthly governance meeting held in a **canoe on the lagoon**. No notes, no phones. Decisions emerge from the rhythm of paddling and the silence between words.

### 8.3 The Tatau Ceremony
Annual ceremony where Guardians receive or renew their **symbolic tatau** — a visual record of their year of service, encoded in Polynesian geometric patterns.

### 8.4 The Awa Ceremony
Traditional kava (*awa*) drinking as community bonding and conflict resolution. The *awa* circle is a **sacred space** — no decisions made outside it are valid.

### 8.5 The Celestial Vigil
Quarterly all-night vigil watching the stars, practicing traditional navigation, and reflecting on the community's "course." What is our *fa'atere* (navigation)? Are we on track?
